📚 Lesson 30: Lookahead and Proposer Knowledge

Spec: phase0/beacon-chain.md → get_seed, compute_activation_exit_epoch
Also: phase0/validator.md → Lookahead section

💡 Core Concept

Every committee assignment and proposer selection depends on randomness—but that randomness must be committed before validators know their duties. Otherwise, an adversary who learns they're the proposer could manipulate the RANDAO reveal to influence future selections.

The spec manages this tension through lookahead parameters:

MIN_SEED_LOOKAHEAD1 epoch MAX_SEED_LOOKAHEAD4 epochs EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR65,536 epochs

These create a delicate balance: validators need enough advance notice to prepare, but not so much that they can game the randomness.

🛠 The Core Mechanism: get_seed

def get_seed(state: BeaconState, epoch: Epoch, domain_type: DomainType) -> Bytes32:
    """Return the seed at ``epoch``."""
    mix = get_randao_mix(
        state, 
        Epoch(epoch + EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR - MIN_SEED_LOOKAHEAD - 1)
    )  # Avoid underflow
    return hash(domain_type + uint_to_bytes(epoch) + mix)

This is the function that powers ALL committee and proposer selection.

The Math Behind the Lookahead

The RANDAO mix index for epoch E is:

E + EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR - MIN_SEED_LOOKAHEAD - 1 = E + 65536 - 1 - 1 = E + 65534 Modulo EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR (how it's stored): (E + 65534) % 65536 = E - 2 (for practical values)

So the seed for epoch E uses the RANDAO mix from epoch E - 2.

Why This Matters

At epoch N, the RANDAO mix is updated by block proposers' reveals throughout epoch N. But duties for epoch N+1 are already determined using the mix from epoch N-1 (frozen before epoch N started).

By the time a proposer in epoch N reveals their RANDAO, they cannot influence their own duties in epoch N+1—those were locked in epochs ago.

⚠️ The Asymmetry: Committees vs Proposers

From validator.md:

"The beacon-chain shufflings are designed to provide a minimum of 1 epoch lookahead on the validator's upcoming committee assignments for attesting. Note that this lookahead does not apply to proposing, which must be checked during the epoch in question."
Duty Lookahead When You Know
Committee 1 epoch Start of epoch N for epoch N+1
Proposer ~0 During epoch N for slots in N

Why the difference? Proposer selection uses the slot number in addition to the seed:

def get_beacon_proposer_index(state: BeaconState) -> ValidatorIndex:
    epoch = get_current_epoch(state)
    seed = hash(get_seed(state, epoch, DOMAIN_BEACON_PROPOSER) + uint_to_bytes(state.slot))
    indices = get_active_validator_indices(state, epoch)
    return compute_proposer_index(state, indices, seed)

The proposer for slot S depends on state.slot—which means you can only compute it once you're in that epoch.

MAX_SEED_LOOKAHEAD and Validator Lifecycle

There's a second lookahead constant: MAX_SEED_LOOKAHEAD = 4.

This appears in activation/exit timing:

def compute_activation_exit_epoch(epoch: Epoch) -> Epoch:
    """Return the epoch during which validator activations 
       and exits initiated in ``epoch`` take effect."""
    return Epoch(epoch + 1 + MAX_SEED_LOOKAHEAD)  # = epoch + 5

Why 4 epochs?

If a validator could exit instantly, they could:

  1. Learn they're the proposer for slot S in epoch E
  2. Trigger an exit before their proposer slot
  3. Avoid penalties for withholding blocks or performing reorg attacks

The 5-epoch delay (1 + MAX_SEED_LOOKAHEAD) ensures that by the time you learn your duties, you're committed to being a validator for all of them.

EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR = 65,536

This is how far back the chain stores RANDAO mixes:

randao_mixes: Vector[Bytes32, EPOCHS_PER_HISTORICAL_VECTOR]

At ~6.4 minutes per epoch, that's ~290 days of history. Why so long?

It enables historical proofs and light client verification back many months. But functionally for lookahead, only the last few epochs matter.

⚠️ Gotchas

1. "Proposer lookahead" is misleading: You can compute future proposers if you know the seed, but you don't know the seed until you're in the epoch. In practice, clients compute all 32 proposers at epoch start.

2. RANDAO manipulation window: The last proposer in an epoch has 1-bit influence over the next epoch's seed. With MIN_SEED_LOOKAHEAD=1, they can't influence their next epoch duties, but they can influence epoch+2. This is the "1-bit bias attack" from Lesson 10.

3. Light clients can't verify future committees: They need the RANDAO mix, which requires tracking all block headers (or trusting a sync committee proof).

❓ Why This Way

The 1-epoch minimum lookahead was a tradeoff:

  • Too short: Validators can't prepare (peer discovery, subnet joining)
  • Too long: RANDAO manipulation becomes more powerful
  • 1 epoch (~6.4 min): Enough time for p2p setup, short enough to limit manipulation

📖 Key Takeaway

Lookahead is about commitment before knowledge. The RANDAO mix that determines your duties was finalized before you could influence it. Your activation/exit takes effect after all your assigned duties. The game theory only works if this timing is airtight.
